Climb Me to the Moon

> Click here to visit the game's Itch.io page

In Climb Me to the Moon one player takes the role of an astronaut trying to climb to the moon. Helping them are floating stars who can push objects and block projectiles to help the astronaut advance.

The game was originally made for a course about rapid prototyping with the prompts 6 player or more multiplayer, and 2 bit color. Development started during a Valentine's game jam which added the additional theme "To the moon and back". The jam took place over a weekend and, by the end, the first prototype of Climb Me to the Moon won the awards for most fun and best interpretation.

The game was then further developed and displayed at Gotland Game Conference 2025 where it recieved the Jury Spotlight award, as well as nominations for the Public Choice, Best Level Design, and Best Art Direction awards.

During its initial development, the biggest challenge was learning the Godot game engine and its scripting language GDScript while working to complete the game in the span of 48 hours. Luckily, in my team of three, one of us had previous experience with the engine and I could ask them if I got stuck or confused. During the development for the main course, we worked on the characters as well as the separate platformer components we would use to build levels. This made it easy to split up work. I created the projectile firing cannons, pair programmed with my teammate to fix issues with the camera and physics, took part in designing levels and tested controller communication with Godot.

As we continued with the game to display it at Gotland Game conference many things were polished. Much of my work focused on the astronaut character. I restructured the astronaut controlling script into an enum state machine to make it easier to work with. The improved stucture the character script allowed me to implement a falling and landing animation. I implemented jump buffering and coyote time to improve the platforming game feel. I also implemented the death animation of the astronaut and used Godot's built in event signals at the end of the animation to trigger the scene to reload.

Another thing I implemented was the player selection screen for determining how many player characters would be active during the game, as well as the logic for deactivating inactive players.
